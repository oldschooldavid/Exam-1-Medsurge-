⚠ Hypertensive Crisis BP ≥180/120 mmHg. Urgency = no end-organ damage. Emergency = end-organ damage (chest pain, confusion, papilledema). Requires IV antihypertensives (nitroprusside, labetalol IV). Lower MAP by no more than 25% in first hour.
✦ NCLEX Pearl Always assess BP in BOTH arms. A difference of >10 mmHg may indicate subclavian stenosis or aortic dissection. Report to provider.

Dietary Management (DASH Diet)

  • Sodium restriction: <2,300 mg/day (ideally <1,500 mg/day)
  • High potassium: bananas, sweet potatoes, spinach
  • Low saturated fat, rich in whole grains, fruits, vegetables
  • Limit alcohol: ≤2 drinks/day (men), ≤1 (women)
  • STOP smoking — nicotine causes vasoconstriction

⚠ Time-Critical — STEMI Protocol Door-to-balloon time goal: <90 minutes. Administer aspirin 325 mg STAT unless contraindicated. Obtain 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes of arrival.
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Troponin Timing Troponin I rises 3–6 hrs after MI, peaks 14–18 hrs, remains elevated 5–7 days. It is the MOST sensitive and specific biomarker for MI. CK-MB rises faster (3–6 hrs) but normalizes sooner (36–48 hrs).

MONA Protocol

  • Morphine — pain relief, reduces preload/afterload (use cautiously; may ↓BP)
  • Oxygen — maintain SpO₂ ≥94%

  • Aspirin — 325 mg PO chewed (antiplatelet)
ℹ Post-MI Nursing Care Bed rest → activity progression. Continuous telemetry monitoring. Measure I&O. Assess for complications: cardiogenic shock, arrhythmias, heart failure, pericarditis (Dressler's syndrome).

ADPIE — CHF Clinical Judgment (Case Study)

  • Assess: Breath sounds (crackles), O₂ sat, weight, edema, JVD, BNP, respiratory rate
  • Diagnose: Decreased Cardiac Output; Excess Fluid Volume; Impaired Gas Exchange; Activity Intolerance
  • Plan: Maintain SpO₂ ≥94%, reduce fluid overload, administer meds, patient education
  • Implement: Administer diuretics, O₂ therapy, daily weights, restrict fluids/sodium, elevate HOB
  • Evaluate: Improved breath sounds, weight loss, improved SpO₂, reduced edema
ℹ Furosemide (Lasix) Nursing Considerations Monitor K⁺ (hypokalemia risk), BP (orthostatic hypotension), urine output. Administer in AM to avoid nocturia. Replace electrolytes as ordered. Teach dietary potassium supplementation.

Pericarditis vs MI Pain

  • Pericarditis: Sharp, positional (better leaning forward), friction rub present, saddle-shaped ST elevation on ECG
  • MI: Crushing, pressure-like, radiating, not positional, focal ST elevation, ↑Troponin
Mitral Valve Surgery Cardiac

Mitral Valve Disorders

  • Mitral Stenosis: Narrowing of valve → ↓flow from LA→LV → pulmonary congestion. Cause: rheumatic fever. S&S: dyspnea, hemoptysis, atrial fibrillation, opening snap + diastolic murmur.
  • Mitral Regurgitation: Backflow into LA → LA enlargement → pulmonary hypertension. Cause: MVP, rheumatic, IE. S&S: holosystolic murmur at apex radiating to axilla, dyspnea, fatigue.

Surgical Interventions

  • Mitral Valve Repair: Preferred when feasible. Preserves native valve.
  • Mitral Valve Replacement: Mechanical (lifelong anticoagulation with warfarin; target INR 2.5–3.5) or Bioprosthetic (no long-term anticoagulation, shorter durability).
ℹ Post-Op Nursing Care — Valve Surgery Monitor for arrhythmias (esp. A-fib). Anticoagulation management. Assess for signs of infection (endocarditis). Monitor hemodynamic status. Activity restrictions. Teach signs of prosthetic valve dysfunction.
✦ NCLEX Pearl Patients with mechanical heart valves require lifelong warfarin therapy. Teach INR monitoring, bleeding precautions, and to avoid foods high in Vitamin K (leafy greens affect INR stability).

Lung Sounds & Assessment Assessment

Normal Breath Sounds

  • Vesicular: Low-pitched, soft, heard over peripheral lung fields. Inspiration > expiration.
  • Bronchovesicular: Medium pitch, heard over mainstem bronchi (between scapulae). Inspiration = expiration.
  • Bronchial/Tracheal: High-pitched, harsh, heard over trachea. Expiration > inspiration.

Abnormal / Adventitious Sounds

  • Crackles (Rales): Discontinuous, popping sounds. Fine crackles = fluid in alveoli (CHF, pneumonia). Coarse crackles = secretions in airways.
  • Wheezes: High-pitched musical sounds, expiratory. Indicate narrowed airways (asthma, COPD, anaphylaxis).
  • Rhonchi: Low-pitched, snoring-like. Secretions in large airways. May clear with cough.
  • Stridor: High-pitched, inspiratory. Upper airway obstruction (croup, foreign body). Medical emergency.
  • Pleural Friction Rub: Grating/leathery sound on inspiration and expiration. Pleural inflammation (pericarditis, pleuritis).
  • Absent Breath Sounds: Pneumothorax, atelectasis, large pleural effusion.
✦ NCLEX Pearl Crackles that DO NOT clear with coughing = fluid (CHF, pulmonary edema). Rhonchi that clear with coughing = secretions. This distinction drives nursing interventions.

Respiratory Assessment Components

  • Rate, rhythm, depth of respirations (normal: 12–20/min)
  • SpO₂ (normal: 95–100%; COPD patients may tolerate 88–92%)
  • Use of accessory muscles, nasal flaring, retractions
  • Color (pallor, cyanosis — peripheral vs central)
  • Cough: productive vs non-productive, sputum color/consistency
  • Auscultate all lung fields (anterior, posterior, lateral)
  • Percussion: resonance (normal), dullness (fluid), hyperresonance (air)
Tracheostomy Care Procedure
⚠ Always Keep at Bedside Spare tracheostomy tube of same size and one size smaller, obturator, 10 mL syringe, suction equipment, bag-valve mask. Critical for emergency reinsertion.

Tracheostomy Care Procedure

  • Perform every 8 hours or as needed
  • Use sterile technique for inner cannula cleaning (use sterile NS or H₂O₂ per facility policy)
  • Change trach ties when soiled; keep one finger between tie and neck
  • Assess stoma site: redness, drainage, skin breakdown
  • Suction PRN (not routinely) — sterile technique, catheter size = 1/2 diameter of trach tube

Suctioning Steps

  • Pre-oxygenate with 100% O₂ for 30–60 seconds
  • Insert catheter WITHOUT suction
  • Apply suction while withdrawing (rotate catheter)
  • Each pass: ≤10–15 seconds
  • Allow rest and re-oxygenate between passes
  • Limit to 3 passes maximum
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Decannulation Emergency If trach tube falls out within 72 hours of surgery (tract not formed): call rapid response, cover stoma with sterile dressing, use bag-valve mask. After tract formed: nurse may reinsert with obturator.

Cuff Management

  • Cuff pressure should be 20–25 mmHg (minimal occlusive volume)
  • Too high → tracheal ischemia/necrosis
  • Too low → aspiration risk
  • Deflate cuff before oral feedings if ordered; assess gag reflex
Influenza Infectious

Signs & Symptoms

  • Abrupt onset fever (101–104°F), chills, rigors
  • Severe myalgia, headache, malaise
  • Dry cough, sore throat, rhinorrhea
  • GI symptoms more common in children (N/V/D)

Treatment & Nursing Interventions

  • Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) — most effective within 48 hours of symptom onset
  • Antipyretics (acetaminophen, ibuprofen) — NOT aspirin in children (Reye's syndrome)
  • Rest, hydration, symptom management
  • Droplet precautions (surgical mask, private room or cohort)
  • Annual vaccination — best prevention
ℹ Isolation Precautions Influenza = Droplet Precautions. Surgical mask within 3 feet of patient. Private room preferred. Patient wears mask when transported. Handwashing is essential.
✦ NCLEX Pearl — High-Risk Populations Highest complication risk: adults ≥65, children <5, pregnant women, immunocompromised, those with chronic conditions (asthma, DM, heart disease). Prioritize vaccination in these groups.

ACE Inhibitors Meds

Common Drugs

Lisinopril, Enalapril, Captopril, Ramipril, Benazepril (-pril suffix)

Mechanism

Inhibit Angiotensin-Converting Enzyme → prevent conversion of Angiotensin I → Angiotensin II → ↓vasoconstriction, ↓aldosterone → ↓BP, ↓afterload, ↓preload.

Indications

  • Hypertension (first-line)
  • Heart failure (reduce mortality)
  • Post-MI (cardioprotective)
  • Diabetic nephropathy (renoprotective)
  • Chronic Kidney Disease

Adverse Effects

  • Dry, persistent cough (most common; due to ↑bradykinin) — switch to ARB if intolerable
  • Hyperkalemia — monitor K⁺
  • First-dose hypotension — start low, go slow; assess orthostatic BP
  • Angioedema (rare but life-threatening) — swelling of lips/tongue/throat; STOP drug immediately
  • Renal impairment — monitor BUN/Creatinine
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy (Category D/X — teratogenic)
⚠ Angioedema — ACE Inhibitor Emergency Angioedema can occur at any time during therapy (even after years of use). Stop drug immediately, administer epinephrine if airway compromised, maintain airway, notify provider.
✦ NCLEX Pearl If a patient has a persistent dry cough on an ACE inhibitor, the appropriate action is to notify the provider and anticipate switching to an ARB (Angiotensin Receptor Blocker) — same benefits, no bradykinin effect.
Beta Blockers Meds

Common Drugs

Metoprolol (selective β₁), Carvedilol (non-selective α/β), Atenolol, Propranolol (non-selective), Bisoprolol (-olol suffix)

Mechanism

Block beta-adrenergic receptors → ↓HR, ↓contractility, ↓BP, ↓renin release. β₁ (cardiac), β₂ (bronchial/vascular).

Indications

  • HTN
  • Angina, post-MI (reduce mortality)
  • Heart failure (carvedilol, metoprolol succinate)
  • Arrhythmias (rate control in A-fib)
  • Migraine prophylaxis

Adverse Effects

  • Bradycardia — hold if HR <60 bpm
  • Hypotension — assess before administering
  • Bronchospasm — avoid non-selective BBs in asthma/COPD (use cardioselective)
  • Fatigue, dizziness, depression
  • Masks hypoglycemia symptoms in diabetics (except diaphoresis)
  • Sexual dysfunction
⚠ Never Abruptly Stop Beta Blockers Abrupt discontinuation → rebound tachycardia, hypertension, angina, MI. Taper dose gradually over 1–2 weeks under medical supervision.
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Hold Parameter Hold beta blocker and notify provider if apical HR is <60 bpm or systolic BP is <90 mmHg. Document and report. Do not administer.

Cardiac Assessment
Heart Sounds · Inspection · Auscultation · Imaging
Cardiac Assessment Techniques Assessment

Inspection

  • Skin color: pallor, cyanosis (peripheral vs central), jaundice
  • Edema: bilateral pitting edema (cardiac), unilateral (lymphedema/DVT)
  • JVD (Jugular Venous Distension): assess at 45° — elevated in R-sided HF, pericardial tamponade
  • Capillary refill: ≤2 seconds normal
  • Clubbing: chronic hypoxia indicator

Auscultation — Heart Sounds

  • S1 ("Lub") — closure of mitral + tricuspid valves; marks onset of systole. Loudest at apex (mitral area).
  • S2 ("Dub") — closure of aortic + pulmonic valves; marks onset of diastole. Loudest at base.
  • S3 (Ventricular Gallop) — early diastole; blood filling a compliant ventricle. Normal in children; ABNORMAL in adults → CHF, volume overload. "Ken-tucky" rhythm.
  • S4 (Atrial Gallop) — late diastole; atrial contraction against stiff ventricle. Associated with HTN, CAD, hypertrophy. "Tenn-es-see" rhythm.

Auscultation Sites (APT PM)

  • Aortic — 2nd ICS, right sternal border
  • Pulmonic — 2nd ICS, left sternal border
  • Tricuspid — 4th ICS, left sternal border
  • Mitral (Apex) — 5th ICS, midclavicular line
  • Erb's Point — 3rd ICS, left sternal border (murmur best heard here)
✦ NCLEX Pearl — S3 vs S4 S3: early diastole, sounds like "Ken-tucky" (Ken=S1, tuc=S2, ky=S3). Indicates volume overload — think CHF. S4: late diastole, sounds like "Tenn-es-see." Indicates stiff/noncompliant ventricle — think HTN or cardiomyopathy.

Murmurs

  • Graded I–VI (I = barely audible, VI = heard without stethoscope)
  • Describe: location, radiation, timing (systolic vs diastolic), quality, intensity
  • Systolic murmur at apex → mitral regurgitation
  • Diastolic murmur at apex → mitral stenosis
  • Pericardial friction rub → pericarditis (scratchy, heard throughout cardiac cycle)

Palpation

  • Point of Maximal Impulse (PMI) — 5th ICS, MCL; displaced laterally in cardiomegaly
  • Peripheral pulses: rate, rhythm, strength (0–4+ scale)
  • Presence of thrills (palpable murmur)
CT Scan & MRI — Nursing Considerations Imaging

CT Scan

  • Uses ionizing radiation + X-rays
  • Faster than MRI; good for acute situations
  • Contrast dye common: assess for contrast allergy, renal function (Creatinine/eGFR)
  • Hold Metformin 48 hrs before/after contrast (lactic acidosis risk)
  • Remove all metallic objects
  • Assess for claustrophobia
  • Cardiac CT: coronary artery calcium scoring, CT angiography (CTA)
ℹ Contrast Pre-Screening Check: Creatinine (goal <1.5 mg/dL), allergy history (shellfish/iodine). Pre-medicate with diphenhydramine and prednisone if prior contrast allergy.

MRI

  • Uses magnetic fields; NO radiation
  • Superior soft tissue contrast; cardiac structure & function
  • Contraindicated: pacemakers, cochlear implants, some metallic implants
  • Gadolinium contrast: safe in most patients; contraindicated in GFR <30 (NSF risk)
  • Remove ALL metallic items (jewelry, hairpins, etc.)
  • Claustrophobia: sedation may be needed
  • Cardiac MRI: assess EF, myocardial viability, pericarditis, cardiomyopathy
⚠ MRI Safety — ABSOLUTE Contraindications Ferromagnetic implants (certain aneurysm clips, older pacemakers, cochlear implants). ALWAYS screen the patient using a standardized MRI screening form before entry.
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Post-Contrast Care After contrast administration: encourage fluids (flush kidneys), monitor urine output, assess for delayed allergic reaction (urticaria, dyspnea) for 30–60 min. Patients with renal impairment are at risk for contrast-induced nephropathy.

Essential Dosage Formulas Calculations
Formula 1 — Basic Dose/Volume

Used to calculate how many mL to administer from a stock solution.

Volume to give = (Desired Dose ÷ Dose on Hand) × Volume on Hand

Example: Order: Metoprolol 50 mg PO. Available: 25 mg/tablet. → (50÷25)×1 = 2 tablets

Formula 2 — Weight-Based Dosing

Common for heparin, enoxaparin, pediatric medications.

Total Dose = Weight (kg) × Dose per kg

Example: Heparin 80 units/kg IV bolus. Patient weighs 70 kg. → 80 × 70 = 5,600 units IV bolus.

Convert lbs to kg: lbs ÷ 2.2 = kg

Formula 3 — IV Flow Rate (mL/hr)

Used for IV pump rate calculation.

Rate (mL/hr) = Total Volume (mL) ÷ Time (hr)

Example: 1000 mL NS over 8 hours → 1000 ÷ 8 = 125 mL/hr

Formula 4 — IV Drip Rate (drops/min — gravity) gtt/min = (Volume to infuse × Drop factor) ÷ Time in minutes

Drop factors: Macrodrip: 10, 15, or 20 gtt/mL | Microdrip: 60 gtt/mL

Example: 500 mL over 4 hrs with 20 gtt/mL set → (500×20) ÷ 240 = 41.6 → 42 gtt/min

Formula 5 — Unit Conversion (mcg/min to mL/hr) mL/hr = (Dose in mcg/min × 60 min × Weight in kg) ÷ Concentration of drug

Used for vasoactive drips (dopamine, dobutamine, nitroglycerin). Know your concentration.

✦ NCLEX Pearl — Rounding Rules Round mL/hr to nearest whole number. Round gtt/min to nearest whole number. Round tablets to nearest half. NEVER round weight-based critical care medications — use precise math.

Normal Intervals

  • PR interval: 0.12–0.20 sec (3–5 small boxes) — AV conduction time
  • QRS complex: ≤0.12 sec (≤3 small boxes) — ventricular depolarization
  • QT interval: ≤0.44 sec — ventricular repolarization (prolonged = risk of Torsades)

Key ECG Changes to Know

  • ST elevation (STEMI): >1 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads — occlusion
  • ST depression: Ischemia (non-occlusive)
  • Saddle-shaped ST elevation: Pericarditis (diffuse, all leads)
  • Peaked T-waves: Hyperkalemia
  • Flattened/inverted T-waves + U-wave: Hypokalemia
  • Prolonged QT: Hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia, medications
