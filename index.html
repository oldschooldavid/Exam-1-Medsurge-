✦ NCLEX PearlAlways assess BP in BOTH arms. A difference of >10 mmHg may indicate subclavian stenosis or aortic dissection. Report to provider.

Dietary Management (DASH Diet)

  • Sodium restriction: <2,300 mg/day (ideally <1,500 mg/day)
  • High potassium: bananas, sweet potatoes, spinach
  • Low saturated fat, rich in whole grains, fruits, vegetables
  • Limit alcohol: ≤2 drinks/day (men), ≤1 (women)
  • STOP smoking — nicotine causes vasoconstriction

ADPIE — CHF Clinical Judgment

  • Assess: Breath sounds, O₂ sat, weight, edema, JVD, BNP, RR
  • Diagnose: Decreased Cardiac Output; Excess Fluid Volume; Impaired Gas Exchange
  • Plan: SpO₂ ≥94%, reduce fluid overload, administer meds, pt education
  • Implement: Diuretics, O₂, daily weights, restrict fluids/sodium, HOB ↑
  • Evaluate: Improved breath sounds, weight loss, improved SpO₂, ↓edema

Mitral Valve SurgeryCardiac

Mitral Valve Disorders

  • Mitral Stenosis: Narrowing → ↓flow LA→LV → pulmonary congestion. Cause: rheumatic fever. S&S: dyspnea, hemoptysis, A-fib, opening snap + diastolic murmur.
  • Mitral Regurgitation: Backflow into LA → enlargement → pulmonary HTN. S&S: holosystolic murmur at apex radiating to axilla, dyspnea, fatigue.

Surgical Interventions

  • Repair: Preferred — preserves native valve.
  • Mechanical Replacement: Lifelong warfarin (INR 2.5–3.5).
  • Bioprosthetic: No long-term anticoagulation, shorter durability.
✦ NCLEX PearlMechanical heart valves require lifelong warfarin. Teach INR monitoring, bleeding precautions, consistent Vitamin K intake (don't eliminate; be consistent).

Anticoagulation Overview

  • Heparin: IV/SQ; monitor aPTT (60–100 sec); antidote: protamine sulfate
  • Lovenox: SQ abdomen; weight-based; no routine aPTT; do NOT expel air bubble
  • Coumadin: PO; monitor PT/INR (goal 2–3); takes 3–5 days; many interactions
Respiratory
Lung Sounds · Tracheostomy · Influenza · Inhaler Medications
Lung Sounds & AssessmentAssessment

Normal Breath Sounds

  • Vesicular: Low-pitched, peripheral lung fields. Inspiration > expiration.
  • Bronchovesicular: Medium pitch, mainstem bronchi. Inspiration = expiration.
  • Bronchial/Tracheal: High-pitched, harsh, over trachea. Expiration > inspiration.

Adventitious Sounds

  • Crackles (Rales): Fine = fluid in alveoli (CHF, pneumonia). Coarse = secretions.
  • Wheezes: High-pitched, expiratory. Narrowed airways (asthma, COPD).
  • Rhonchi: Low-pitched, snoring. Large airway secretions. May clear with cough.
  • Stridor: High-pitched, inspiratory. Upper airway obstruction. EMERGENCY.
  • Pleural Friction Rub: Grating on inspiration and expiration. Pleuritis/pericarditis.
✦ NCLEX PearlCrackles NOT clearing with coughing = fluid (CHF, pulmonary edema). Rhonchi clearing with coughing = secretions.
Tracheostomy CareProcedure
⚠ Always Keep at BedsideSpare trach tube (same size + one size smaller), obturator, 10 mL syringe, suction equipment, bag-valve mask.

Suctioning Steps

  • Pre-oxygenate with 100% O₂ for 30–60 seconds
  • Insert catheter WITHOUT suction
  • Apply suction while withdrawing (rotate catheter)
  • Each pass: ≤10–15 seconds; limit to 3 passes
  • Allow rest and re-oxygenate between passes
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Decannulation EmergencyIf trach falls out <72 hrs post-op: call rapid response, cover stoma, use bag-valve mask. After tract formed: nurse may reinsert with obturator.

Cuff Management

  • Cuff pressure: 20–25 mmHg
  • Too high → tracheal ischemia/necrosis
  • Too low → aspiration risk
InfluenzaInfectious

S&S

  • Abrupt fever (101–104°F), chills, rigors
  • Severe myalgia, HA, malaise
  • Dry cough, sore throat, rhinorrhea

Treatment

  • Oseltamivir (Tamiflu) — within 48 hrs of symptom onset
  • NOT aspirin in children (Reye's syndrome)
  • Droplet precautions (surgical mask within 3 ft, private room)
✦ NCLEX Pearl — High-Risk PopulationsAdults ≥65, children <5, pregnant women, immunocompromised, chronic conditions. Prioritize vaccination.

ACE InhibitorsMeds

Drugs: Lisinopril, Enalapril, Captopril, Ramipril (-pril suffix)

Adverse Effects

  • Dry persistent cough (↑bradykinin) — switch to ARB if intolerable
  • Hyperkalemia — monitor K⁺
  • First-dose hypotension — start low
  • Angioedema — STOP immediately, epinephrine if airway compromised
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy
✦ NCLEX PearlPersistent dry cough on ACE inhibitor → notify provider, anticipate switch to ARB (same benefits, no bradykinin cough).
Beta BlockersMeds

Drugs: Metoprolol (selective β₁), Carvedilol (α/β), Atenolol, Propranolol (-olol suffix)

Adverse Effects

  • Bradycardia — hold if HR <60 bpm
  • Hypotension — assess before administering
  • Bronchospasm — avoid non-selective in asthma/COPD
  • Masks hypoglycemia (except diaphoresis)
⚠ Never Abruptly Stop Beta BlockersRebound tachycardia, hypertension, angina, MI. Taper over 1–2 weeks.
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Hold ParameterHold and notify if apical HR <60 bpm or systolic BP <90 mmHg.

Cardiac Assessment
Heart Sounds · Inspection · Auscultation · Imaging
Cardiac Assessment TechniquesAssessment

Heart Sounds

  • S1 ("Lub") — mitral + tricuspid closure; onset of systole. Loudest at apex.
  • S2 ("Dub") — aortic + pulmonic closure; onset of diastole. Loudest at base.
  • S3 (Ventricular Gallop) — early diastole. ABNORMAL in adults → CHF. "Ken-tucky."
  • S4 (Atrial Gallop) — late diastole; stiff ventricle. HTN, cardiomyopathy. "Tenn-es-see."

Auscultation Sites (APT ME)

  • Aortic — 2nd ICS, right sternal border
  • Pulmonic — 2nd ICS, left sternal border
  • Tricuspid — 4th ICS, left sternal border
  • Mitral — 5th ICS, midclavicular line
  • Erb's Point — 3rd ICS, left sternal border
✦ NCLEX Pearl — S3 vs S4S3 = "Ken-tucky" = volume overload = CHF. S4 = "Tenn-es-see" = stiff ventricle = HTN/cardiomyopathy.
CT Scan & MRI — Nursing ConsiderationsImaging

CT Scan

  • Ionizing radiation; faster
  • Check Creatinine/eGFR for contrast
  • Hold Metformin 48 hrs before/after contrast
  • Pre-screen for iodine/shellfish allergy
ℹ Contrast Pre-ScreeningCheck Cr <1.5 mg/dL; pre-medicate with diphenhydramine + prednisone if prior contrast allergy.

MRI

  • Magnetic fields; NO radiation
  • Absolute CI: pacemakers, cochlear implants, ferromagnetic implants
  • Gadolinium: CI in GFR <30 (NSF risk)
  • Remove ALL metal items
⚠ MRI — Absolute CIsAlways use standardized MRI screening form before entry.
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Post-Contrast CareEncourage fluids, monitor urine output, assess for delayed allergic reaction 30–60 min post-contrast.
Dosage Calculations
3 Dosage Questions Expected
ℹ Exam Note3 dosage calculation questions on the exam. Types: basic dose/volume, weight-based, IV flow rate.
Essential Dosage FormulasCalculations
Formula 1 — Basic Dose/VolumeVolume to give = (Desired Dose ÷ Dose on Hand) × Volume on Hand

Example: Order: Metoprolol 50 mg. Available: 25 mg/tablet. → (50÷25)×1 = 2 tablets

Formula 2 — Weight-Based DosingTotal Dose = Weight (kg) × Dose per kg

Example: Heparin 80 units/kg. Patient 70 kg. → 80×70 = 5,600 units IV bolus.
Convert: lbs ÷ 2.2 = kg

Formula 3 — IV Flow Rate (mL/hr)Rate (mL/hr) = Total Volume (mL) ÷ Time (hr)

Example: 1000 mL NS over 8 hrs → 1000÷8 = 125 mL/hr

Formula 4 — IV Drip Rate (gtt/min)gtt/min = (Volume × Drop factor) ÷ Time in minutes

Drop factors: Macrodrip: 10, 15, or 20 gtt/mL | Microdrip: 60 gtt/mL

✦ NCLEX Pearl — Rounding RulesRound mL/hr and gtt/min to nearest whole number. Round tablets to nearest half. NEVER round critical care weight-based meds.

ECG · Pre/Post-Procedure Care
12-Lead ECG BasicsProcedure

Normal Intervals

  • PR interval: 0.12–0.20 sec (AV conduction)
  • QRS: ≤0.12 sec (ventricular depolarization)
  • QT: ≤0.44 sec (prolonged = Torsades risk)

Key ECG Changes

  • ST elevation (STEMI): >1 mm in ≥2 contiguous leads
  • Saddle-shaped ST elevation: Pericarditis
  • Peaked T-waves: Hyperkalemia
  • Flattened T + U-wave: Hypokalemia
  • Prolonged QT: Hypomagnesemia, hypokalemia
✦ NCLEX Pearl — STEMI LeadsAnterior → V1–V4. Inferior → II, III, aVF. Lateral → I, aVL, V5–V6. Posterior → reciprocal V1–V2.

Blood Disorders
Anemia Types · Sickle Cell · Iron Deficiency · B12 · Folic Acid · Aplastic · Leukemia
📋 Prof. Note — Dr. ApostolDiagnosis uses CBC with Differentials. Low Hgb and Hct = low O₂ carrying capacity. Always ask: What is causing the issue? That determines what you treat and what you give.
Blood Disorders OverviewHematology

Common Blood Disorders

  • Anemia (multiple types — see below)
  • Sickle cell disease
  • Clotting disorders (thrombocytopenia)
  • Leukemia (WBC cancer)
  • Aplastic anemia (bone marrow failure)

Key Diagnostic Labs

  • CBC with Differentials — primary diagnostic tool
  • Low Hemoglobin (Hgb) + Low Hematocrit (Hct) = ↓O₂ carrying capacity
  • Normal Hgb: Men 13.5–17.5 g/dL; Women 12–15.5 g/dL
  • Normal Hct: Men 41–53%; Women 36–46%

Universal Treatment Principles

  • Assess tissue perfusion — elevated HR compensates for ↓O₂ capacity
  • Blood transfusion in severe cases
  • Treat the underlying deficiency (iron, B12, folic acid)
  • Identify and treat the root cause first
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Anemia Assessment PriorityIn anemia, the nurse's FIRST action is to assess tissue perfusion (HR, SpO₂, skin color, cap refill). Compensatory tachycardia is an early sign — elevated HR = the heart working harder to deliver less oxygen.

Causes by Population

  • Infants (4–6 mo): Maternal supply diminishes → start iron-fortified cereal (rice)
  • Toddlers: Too much milk, food jags (eating only one item), not enough food variety
  • Teens: Poor diet, rapid growth, menses in females

ℹ Prof. Note — Iron Pills Side EffectIron pills cause constipation. Teach patients to drink lots of fluids and increase fiber. Stools may turn black/tarry — this is expected and NOT a sign of GI bleeding.

Vitamin B12 Deficiency (Pernicious Anemia)Hematology

Pathophysiology

Vitamin B12 is required for RBC production. Deficiency = impaired DNA synthesis → large, immature RBCs (megaloblastic anemia).

Causes

  • Poor dietary intake (vegans, extreme diets)
  • Lack of intrinsic factor (pernicious anemia — autoimmune)
  • Bowel resection, tapeworm, diverticulitis
  • Malabsorption syndromes

Classic Signs & Symptoms

  • Pallor, jaundice, fatigue, weight loss
  • Glossitis — smooth, beefy red tongue (hallmark finding)
  • Paresthesias of hands and feet (neurological involvement)

Treatment

  • Dietary B12: animal proteins, eggs, nuts, dairy, dried beans, citrus, leafy greens
  • IM B12 injections (cyanocobalamin) if intrinsic factor absent — cannot absorb PO
  • Lifelong injections if pernicious anemia
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Beefy Red TongueSmooth, beefy red tongue (glossitis) = hallmark of B12 deficiency. If you see this on a test question → think B12/pernicious anemia. Also look for neurological symptoms (numbness, tingling) which do NOT occur in iron deficiency.
Folic Acid Deficiency AnemiaHematology

Causes

  • Poor nutrition — diet lacking green leafy vegetables, liver, yeast, citrus, dried beans, nuts
  • Malabsorption (Crohn's disease)
  • Chronic alcohol abuse — major cause of dietary-based anemias
  • Certain drugs (methotrexate, phenytoin)
  • Pregnancy (↑demand)

Treatment

  • Folic acid supplementation (PO)
  • Dietary sources: leafy greens, liver, yeast, citrus, beans, nuts
  • Address alcohol abuse if applicable
  • Folic acid supplementation critical in pregnancy (prevents neural tube defects)
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Folic Acid vs B12Both cause megaloblastic anemia, but B12 deficiency causes neurological symptoms (paresthesias) — folic acid does NOT. This distinction is tested frequently.
Aplastic AnemiaHematology

Pathophysiology

  • Bone marrow fails to produce RBCs — injury to immature precursor cells
  • Often occurs with Leukopenia (↓WBC) and Thrombocytopenia (↓platelets)
  • Pancytopenia = deficiency in ALL cell types (RBCs + WBCs + platelets)

Causes

  • Long-term exposure to toxic agents, drugs (chloramphenicol, chemotherapy)
  • Radiation, infection (viral)
  • Autoimmune destruction of stem cells

Treatment

  • Blood transfusion (RBCs, platelets)
  • Chemotherapy, immunosuppression, steroids
  • Bone marrow/stem cell transplant (curative)
  • Treatment is largely trial and error
⚠ Pancytopenia Risks↓RBCs = anemia (fatigue, ↑HR). ↓WBCs = infection risk (neutropenic precautions). ↓Platelets = bleeding risk (no IM injections, soft toothbrush, electric razor, avoid NSAIDs).
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Pancytopenia PriorityWith pancytopenia, the HIGHEST priority concern is infection (↓WBC). Even though all three cell lines are low, neutropenia puts the patient at greatest immediate risk. Implement neutropenic precautions.
Leukemia & Chronic Renal Failure AnemiaHematology

Leukemia

  • Cancer — uncontrolled production of immature WBCs
  • Bone marrow is overcrowded → normal cell production decreases
  • Causes anemia (↓RBCs) AND thrombocytopenia (↓platelets)
  • Diagnosed by bone marrow biopsy, CBC, flow cytometry

Chronic Renal Failure → Anemia

  • Erythropoietin (EPO) is a hormone made by the kidneys that stimulates RBC production in bone marrow
  • In renal failure → ↓EPO → ↓RBC production → anemia
  • Treatment: Epoetin alfa (Epogen) — synthetic EPO injection
✦ NCLEX Pearl — EPO and Renal FailureAnemia of chronic kidney disease is caused by insufficient erythropoietin production. The treatment is NOT iron (unless iron-deficient) — it is erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (epoetin alfa/darbepoetin alfa).

MyocarditisHeart Inflammation

Definition & Causes

  • Inflammation of the myocardium (heart muscle)
  • Most common cause: viral infection (Coxsackievirus B, COVID-19)
  • Also: bacterial, autoimmune, drugs (cocaine, chemotherapy)

Signs & Symptoms

  • Chest pain, fever, fatigue
  • Cardiac rhythm disturbances (palpitations, arrhythmias)
  • Signs of heart failure (dyspnea, edema) if severe
  • ↑Troponin, ↑ESR, ↑CRP

Diagnosis & Treatment

  • Echo (assess function), ECG, MRI, endomyocardial biopsy (definitive)
  • Anti-inflammatory medications (NSAIDs, steroids)
  • Treat underlying cause (antivirals, antibiotics)
  • Rest — activity restriction is critical (exercise can worsen)
  • Heart failure management if needed (diuretics, ACE inhibitors)
✦ NCLEX PearlActivity restriction is the MOST important intervention in myocarditis. Exercise in the acute phase can trigger fatal arrhythmias. Athletes diagnosed with myocarditis must stop all athletic activity.

RhinosinusitisRespiratory

Definition & Causes

  • Inflammation of sinus mucosa (nasal passages + sinuses)
  • Causes: viral infection (most common), bacterial, allergies, structural obstruction
  • Acute (<4 weeks) vs Chronic (>12 weeks)

Signs & Symptoms

  • Facial pain/pressure/fullness (especially around eyes, forehead, cheeks)
  • Nasal congestion, purulent discharge
  • Fever, headache, fatigue
  • Postnasal drip → cough

Treatment

  • Viral: supportive — fluids, rest, saline irrigation
  • Bacterial: antibiotics (amoxicillin first-line)
  • Decongestants (oxymetazoline — max 3 days to avoid rebound)
  • Antihistamines (if allergic component)
  • Nasal corticosteroids (fluticasone) — reduce inflammation
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Rebound CongestionNasal decongestant sprays (oxymetazoline/Afrin) should NEVER be used longer than 3 days. Prolonged use causes rhinitis medicamentosa (rebound congestion) — worse than the original congestion.
Age-Related Respiratory Changes & AssessmentRespiratory

Aging Effects on Respiratory System

  • Stiff airways, decreased lung elasticity (reduced elastic recoil)
  • Reduced alveolar surface area → ↓gas exchange efficiency
  • Weakened respiratory muscles (↓inspiratory force)
  • Blunted cough/gag reflex → ↑aspiration risk
  • Higher infection risk (especially pneumonia)
  • Kyphosis → barrel chest appearance even without disease

Respiratory Assessment — Case Study (Prof. Notes)

📋 Case Study — 60-year-old with SOBPatient: 60 y/o, SpO₂ 86%, fever, cough with green sputum.
Abnormal findings: Green sputum = likely bacterial infection. SpO₂ 86% = severe hypoxemia.
Diagnostics: Sputum culture, Chest X-ray, CBC, ABG.
Treatments: O₂ therapy, IV fluids, antibiotics, bronchodilator (albuterol), antipyretic (acetaminophen), steroids (anti-inflammatory).

Signs of Respiratory Distress

  • Pursed-lip breathing (COPD — ↑expiratory pressure)
  • Tripod position (leaning forward on arms to expand thorax)
  • Nasal flaring, intercostal retractions
  • Cyanosis — peripheral (fingers/toes) or central (lips/tongue = more severe)
  • Clubbing of fingers (chronic hypoxia >3 months)
  • Increased AP (anterior-posterior) chest diameter — barrel chest (COPD)
Endotracheal Tube & Ventilator Weaning PhasesRespiratory

Airway Devices

  • Endotracheal Tube (ETT): Temporary airway — typically used up to ~14 days
  • Tracheostomy: Long-term airway — created when ETT needed >14 days
  • Getting around with a trach is better/more comfortable than with an ETT

Ventilator Weaning Phases (Prof. Notes)

  • Phase 1 — Full Support (AC/CMV): Ventilator does all the breathing for the patient
  • Phase 2 — IMV/SIMV: Patient takes some breaths + ventilator delivers set rate. Patient gets ½ sedation.
  • Phase 3 — CPAP: Patient breathes on their own but ETT still in place. Ventilator provides positive pressure support only. Minimal/no sedation.
  • Extubation: Remove ETT when patient can breathe independently
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Weaning Readiness CriteriaBefore extubation assess: SpO₂ ≥95% on low FiO₂, RR <25/min, patient following commands, strong cough/gag reflex, hemodynamically stable, alert enough to protect airway.

The Rights of Medication Administration & 3 ChecksMed Admin

10 Rights of Medication Administration

  • 1. Right Patient — 2 identifiers (name + DOB or MRN)
  • 2. Right Drug — verify medication name
  • 3. Right Dose — calculate and verify
  • 4. Right Route — PO, IV, IM, SQ, etc.
  • 5. Right Time — on schedule; STAT = immediate
  • 6. Right Documentation — document immediately after giving
  • 7. Right Reason — know why the patient is getting this drug
  • 8. Right Response — evaluate therapeutic effect + side effects
  • 9. Right to Refuse — patient has the right to refuse; document and notify
  • 10. Right Education — inform patient what med is for and side effects

The 3 Checks

  • Check 1: When removing medication from storage (Pyxis)
  • Check 2: When preparing it in the patient's room
  • Check 3: Before giving it to the patient (at bedside)
⚠ Before Giving Any MedAssess patient health status. Review complete medication history (Rx, OTC, alcohol, herbal). Ask about ALL allergies — medications, latex, tapes, foods.
📐 During Administration1. Identify patient (name + DOB/MRN). 2. Inform about medication + side effects. 3. Administer safely. 4. Provide supporting interventions. 5. Document time, dose, response. 6. Evaluate effectiveness.
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Right Patient IdentificationALWAYS use 2 patient identifiers before giving ANY medication. Ask the patient to state their name and date of birth — do NOT just ask "Are you Mr. Jones?" (patient may say yes even if confused). Check the ID band.
Documentation & Controlled SubstancesMed Admin

Documentation in the MAR

  • Record all medications in the MAR (Medication Administration Record)
  • Document IMMEDIATELY after giving — never before or ahead of time
  • For pain meds: document pain score BEFORE and AFTER to evaluate effectiveness
  • Record any adverse reactions, delays, or withheld doses with reason
  • Never document for another nurse

Controlled Substances

  • Stored in a secure locked cabinet (Pyxis)
  • Counted every shift — nurse-to-nurse count at shift change
  • Wasting must be witnessed AND documented by a second nurse
  • Any discrepancy must be reported immediately to charge nurse/pharmacy
  • Never take partial doses and pocket the rest

IV Medications — Student Scope (First Semester)

  • Students do NOT administer or flush IVs in first semester
  • You may observe a nurse giving IV medications
  • You may assess IVs for correct medication and rate
  • Always ask your clinical instructor before touching any IV line
⚠ Orthostatic Hypotension — Post-MedicationAfter giving antihypertensives, diuretics, or any BP-lowering drug, assess for orthostatic hypotension: measure BP lying → sitting → standing. Warn patient to change positions slowly. Implement fall precautions.
✦ NCLEX Pearl — Medication ErrorsIf a medication error occurs: FIRST ensure patient safety (assess for adverse effects). Then notify the provider. Document objectively. Complete an incident report (this is NOT in the MAR). Never falsify records.
